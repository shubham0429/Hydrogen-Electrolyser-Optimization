ABSTRACT

Techno-Economic Optimization of PEM and Alkaline Electrolyser Systems:
A 15-Year Comparative Simulation Study

This thesis presents a comprehensive techno-economic comparison of Proton Exchange
Membrane (PEM) and Alkaline water electrolysis technologies through 15-year dynamic
simulations coupled with multi-objective optimization. A Python-based simulation
framework was developed to model hourly electrolyser operation under variable renewable
energy input (132 MW combined wind-solar), incorporating electrochemical polarization
models, component-level degradation, stack replacement scheduling, and discounted
cash flow economics.

Exhaustive grid search optimization across 600 configurations (electrolyser size ×
storage capacity × renewable energy fraction) identified optimal systems using a
three-criteria hierarchy: minimum levelized cost of hydrogen (LCOH), ≥95% demand
satisfaction, and specific energy consumption as tiebreaker. The PEM optimum (35 MW,
12,000 kg storage) achieves €9.06/kg LCOH at 41.1% capacity factor, while the
Alkaline optimum (20 MW, 10,000 kg storage) achieves €4.86/kg at 73.0% capacity
factor — a 46% cost advantage driven by lower CAPEX (€1,150 vs. €1,950/kW), higher
utilization, and longer stack lifetime (80,000 vs. 60,000 hours).

Monte Carlo uncertainty analysis (10,000 samples) quantifies LCOH ranges of
€6.24–12.64/kg (PEM) and €4.35–7.04/kg (Alkaline). Sensitivity analysis identifies
electricity price as the dominant cost lever for both technologies. The results
provide a technology decision framework for industrial hydrogen project developers,
demonstrating that Alkaline electrolysis offers superior economics for steady-state
applications while PEM retains advantages in dynamic response scenarios.

Keywords: Water electrolysis, PEM, Alkaline, LCOH, techno-economic optimization,
hydrogen production, renewable energy integration, 15-year simulation
